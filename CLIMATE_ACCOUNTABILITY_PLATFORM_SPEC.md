# Portland Climate Accountability Platform — Phase 1 Specification

## A Portland Lab Pilot for the Chief Sustainability Officer

-----

## Why This Exists

On February 25, 2026, City Auditor Simone Rede published a climate justice audit that found Portland lacks the infrastructure to follow through on its own climate commitments. City Administrator Raymond Lee accepted all five recommendations the same day.

This platform is the direct technical response to that audit. It gives the Chief Sustainability Officer the tool she needs to do the job the audit says isn't getting done — and it gives the public the transparency the audit says is missing.

-----

## The Five Audit Findings and What They Require

The audit made five recommendations. Every one of them requires data infrastructure that does not currently exist. This platform builds that infrastructure.

### Audit Recommendation 1: Centralized leadership and coordination

**The finding:** "The Chief Sustainability Officer is positioned centrally within the City but does not have a direct link to bureaus." Bureau staff report to the City Administrator through deputy city administrators, not to the CSO. The CSO cannot enforce bureau accountability for climate work. Past coordination depended on "committed individuals doing their best and relying on the relationships they've built as a substitute for a more defined coordination structure."

**What the platform builds:** A Bureau Climate Scorecard that makes every bureau's climate performance visible without requiring the CSO to have line authority. When the data shows that a bureau has zero workplan actions on track, the CSO doesn't need enforcement power — the data does the enforcing. The scorecard creates accountability through transparency rather than hierarchy.

**Specifically:** For each of the 12+ bureaus with climate responsibilities (PBOT, BES, PWB, PP&R, BPS, PP&D, PF&R, PBEM, OMF, and others), the platform tracks: how many workplan actions are assigned to that bureau, the status of each action (achieved, ongoing, delayed), what funding is allocated, what milestones have been hit, and the trend over time. The CSO can walk into any meeting with the City Administrator or City Council's Climate, Resilience, and Land Use Committee and show a single screen that answers: "Which bureaus are meeting their climate commitments and which aren't?"

This also addresses the audit's finding that "more than half of the actions in the 2022 Plan involved more than one bureau" and that cross-bureau coordination was informal. The platform shows which actions require multiple bureaus and whether all responsible parties are contributing.

### Audit Recommendation 2: Factor climate cost considerations into the budget process

**The finding:** "Climate work is not fully integrated into the City's budget process." The Sustainability Office is exploring climate budgeting, a C40 Cities best practice that would embed climate targets into budget decisions. But no tool exists to connect climate actions to budget allocations.

**What the platform builds:** A Climate-Budget Alignment view that maps every workplan action to its funding source, allocated amount, spent amount, and funding gap. The audit found that "around a quarter of all actions" had staffing or funding gaps exceeding $500,000, and "for most, the gaps were substantially higher." But these gaps are buried in a PDF workplan — they're not visible during budget season.

**Specifically:** The platform encodes the workplan's resource gap classifications ($, $$, $$$, $$$$, $$$$$, and FTE gaps) alongside actual budget allocations. It shows: which actions are funded, which are unfunded, which are funded through PCEF, which are funded through general fund, and the total climate funding gap. During budget season, the CSO can present a single view: "Here are the climate commitments Council has adopted, here is what is funded, here is the gap, and here is what the audit says about the cost of delayed action on life and safety."

The platform also tracks PCEF allocations to bureau projects separately from PCEF community grants — directly addressing the audit's finding that "some community and business groups also told us that the City has not been transparent enough about how PCEF funding is used when allocated to bureaus." Every dollar of PCEF flowing to a city bureau versus a community organization is visible.

### Audit Recommendation 3: Climate adaptation goals and strategy

**The finding:** "The 2022 Plan does not have specific goals for climate adaptation." The plan has mitigation targets (50% below 1990 by 2030, net-zero by 2050) but no equivalent adaptation goals. Around half the workplan's actions are adaptation-focused, but there's no framework to measure whether adaptation is working.

**What the platform builds:** A structured separation of mitigation and adaptation actions with distinct tracking frameworks. For mitigation, the platform tracks against quantified emissions targets. For adaptation, it creates a framework for the CSO and the Sustainability and Climate Commission to define measurable adaptation goals tied to Portland's specific climate risks (extreme heat, wildfire smoke, flooding, power outages) and track progress against them.

**Specifically:** The 43 workplan actions are categorized into decarbonization (22 actions across electricity, buildings, transportation, industry, land use, embodied carbon, and cross-sector) and resilience (21 actions across flooding, trees, natural resources, wildfire, health impacts, resilience hubs, infrastructure, and emergency planning). The mitigation side connects to the emissions inventory trajectory. The adaptation side connects to a risk-based framework that the platform makes structurally possible — once the Climate Change Risk Assessment currently in progress is complete, its findings can be mapped directly to adaptation actions and goals.

This also positions the platform as the backbone of the next climate action plan the CSO is developing in 2026. Rather than starting from scratch, the new plan can be built on top of the tracking infrastructure that already exists.

### Audit Recommendation 4: Transparent prioritization and sufficient action detail

**The finding:** Climate actions "lacked sufficient detail to facilitate implementation." Specifically: 21% of actions have no clear timeframes. 26% have no information about estimated staffing or funding shortages. 100% of actions identify responsible bureaus but none clearly identify external partners or define their roles. The prioritization criteria were internal and not communicated publicly.

**What the platform builds:** A Workplan Tracker that encodes every action with full structured metadata — filling in the detail gaps the audit identified. Every action has: responsible bureau(s), timeframe, funding source, funding gap, status, milestones, external partners, and the prioritization criteria (whether it maps to the Climate Emergency Declaration, the 100% Renewable Energy Resolution, or the 2035 Comprehensive Plan).

**Specifically:** The tracker transforms the workplan from a static PDF into a living, queryable database. Each of the 43 actions becomes a structured record with a complete update history. The public can see not just "what was promised" but "what has happened since, when, and by whom." This directly addresses the audit's finding that "there are no plans to transparently communicate progress on actions because the plan is expired." The platform continues tracking progress through the interim period between the expired 2022 Plan and the new plan being developed in 2026-2027.

The audit specifically flagged that "transparently communicating how the City prioritizes climate actions could help build trust by demonstrating that decision-making is fair." The platform makes the prioritization visible: each action shows whether it was flagged as a priority (★ designation), which policy directives it maps to, its resource gap, and its co-benefits (the audit noted that "around a quarter of the actions in the 2022 Plan have co-benefits for improving public health").

### Audit Recommendation 5: Community engagement strategy

**The finding:** Community groups feel burdened by repeated requests for the same information. The City included limited community input in developing the 2022 Plan. Community members want more meaningful engagement, including "sharing more climate work information with the community, clarifying how community groups can engage in climate work, and involving community groups in decision making."

**What the platform builds:** A public transparency layer that gives every Portland resident access to the same climate performance data that the CSO and City Council see. This is the most fundamental form of community engagement: giving people the information they need to participate meaningfully.

**Specifically:** The platform's public dashboard answers, in plain language: Is Portland on track to meet its emissions targets? (Trajectory chart showing current emissions vs. 2030 and 2050 goals.) Which climate actions are happening and which aren't? (Workplan tracker with status for all 43 actions.) Where is the climate money going? (PCEF tracker showing grants by organization, amount, neighborhood, and program area.) Which bureaus are doing their part? (Bureau scorecard.) What has the Sustainability and Climate Commission recommended, and what happened? (SCC activity tracker.)

The platform also includes embeddable components — any community organization, journalist, or council member can embed specific charts or tables on their own sites. This distributes the data rather than centralizing it, and it means community groups can use the platform's data for their own advocacy without needing to file public records requests or parse PDFs.

## What Gets Built in Phase 1

Phase 1 is a 6-8 week build that delivers the four core views that respond to the audit. It uses the existing Portland Commons dashboard architecture (Next.js, PostgreSQL, Python ETL).

### View 1: Workplan Tracker

A table of all 43 actions with columns for: action number, title, sector, lead bureau(s), declaration alignment (★), status (achieved/ongoing/delayed), fiscal year, resource gap, PCEF-funded (yes/no), and multi-bureau (yes/no). Each row expands to show: full description, all responsible bureaus, target detail, status history with dates and narrative, funding detail, and related PCEF grants.

Filters: by sector (14 categories), by bureau, by status, by funding source, by declaration alignment, by mitigation vs. adaptation.

Summary bar at top: X achieved, Y ongoing, Z delayed — with the same percentages the audit cited (13% achieved, 79% ongoing, 9% delayed as of the final progress report, updated as new data comes in).

### View 2: Bureau Scorecard

Grid of bureau cards. Each shows: bureau name, number of assigned actions, actions by status (achieved/ongoing/delayed), number of cross-bureau actions, total resource gap estimate, PCEF funding received, and an overall performance indicator.

Click any bureau to see its full action list with detail. This view exists nowhere else in city government. It answers the question the audit says the CSO cannot currently answer: which bureaus are accountable for what, and whether they're performing.

### View 3: Climate Finance Tracker

Three sections:

**Funding by source:** What percentage of workplan actions are funded through PCEF, general fund, federal/state grants, or unfunded. Directly addresses the audit's finding about resource gaps.

**PCEF allocation split:** Bureau projects vs. community grants, by fiscal year. Shows the $740M flowing to six bureaus through mid-2029 versus the community grants program. Addresses the audit's finding about transparency of PCEF use.

**PCEF interest diversions:** The ~$25M in PCEF-generated interest redirected to fill general fund budget gaps. This is the single most politically sensitive data point in Portland's climate landscape, and making it visible is exactly what transparency means.

### View 4: Emissions Trajectory

Line chart: Multnomah County total emissions from 1990 to 2023 (latest available), with target lines for the 2030 goal (50% below 1990) and the 2050 goal (net zero). Shows the gap between current trajectory and target. The audit says Portland has reduced 26% from 1990 levels and needs to double annual reductions to hit 2030. This chart makes that gap viscerally clear.

Stacked area chart: Emissions by sector over time. Shows which sectors are driving reductions and which are lagging. This is critical because the workplan's decarbonization actions map to specific sectors — if transportation emissions aren't declining, the transportation actions need more attention.

Per-capita trend line for context.

## What This Enables That Currently Doesn't Exist

**For the Chief Sustainability Officer:** A tool to walk into budget meetings, City Administrator briefings, and council committee sessions with live data rather than annual PDFs. The ability to say "here is exactly which bureaus are behind on their commitments and here is exactly how much it would cost to fund the unfunded actions" — backed by a public URL, not a private memo.

**For the Sustainability and Climate Commission:** A data foundation for developing the next climate action plan. Instead of starting from scratch, the 20 commissioners can see the complete performance history of the 2022 Plan and use it to inform what goes into the next plan. The platform also tracks SCC recommendations and whether they're acted on — giving the volunteer commissioners the ability to see their own impact.

**For the City Administrator:** A Portland Lab pilot product that demonstrates the concept of civic transparency infrastructure. The climate dashboard becomes the proof of concept for extending the same approach to permitting, housing, homelessness, and budget transparency.

**For City Council's Climate Committee:** Real-time data for the committee co-chairs (currently Councilors Novick and others) to exercise oversight. The audit found that "there are no plans to transparently communicate progress on actions because the plan is expired." This platform fills that gap during the 2026-2027 interim period.

**For the public:** The ability to answer "is my city actually doing what it promised on climate?" without reading 80-page PDFs. Embeddable widgets for journalists and community organizations. CSV exports for researchers. A permanent, updatable public record of what was committed and what happened.

**For the next climate action plan:** The platform becomes the publication and tracking medium for the plan itself. Instead of another PDF that gets an annual progress report, the next plan launches as a living system with built-in status tracking, bureau accountability, and public transparency from day one.

## Build Sequence

**Week 1-2:** Database schema creation. Manual encoding of all 43 workplan actions with full metadata. Ingest three progress reports to populate status history. Encode emissions inventory data from BPS dashboard (1990-2023).

**Week 3-4:** Workplan Tracker view (table with filters, expand-to-detail, status summary bar). Bureau Scorecard view (bureau cards, assigned actions, performance indicators). Wire up API routes.

**Week 5-6:** Climate Finance Tracker (funding gap analysis, PCEF allocation split, PCEF interest diversions). Emissions Trajectory view (line chart with targets, stacked area by sector, per-capita).

**Week 7-8:** Story factory rules for climate section. Embed/export functionality. Mobile optimization. SCC activity tracker (meeting records, recommendations). Polish, QA, and deployment.

**Deliverable:** A live, public URL that responds to every finding in the February 2026 audit — built during the exact window when the CSO is developing the next climate action plan and the City Administrator has publicly accepted the audit's recommendations.

## Why This Should Be Phase 1 of Portland Lab

The climate dashboard is the ideal first product for Portland Lab because:

1. **The political mandate already exists.** The auditor published the business case. The City Administrator accepted the recommendations. The CSO has publicly said she needs this. There is no persuasion required — only execution.
2. **The data is available.** Unlike other potential Portland Lab projects that require new data collection or interagency agreements, the climate data exists in published PDFs, an existing BPS dashboard, and the PCEF portal. It needs to be structured and unified, not created.
3. **The audience is defined.** The CSO, the SCC, the Climate Committee, journalists, and climate-engaged residents — a clear, motivated user base who will use the product immediately.
4. **It demonstrates the pattern.** The same architecture — encode government commitments as structured data, track performance, make it public — extends to every other domain Portland Lab might address. Permitting timelines. Housing production. Homelessness services. Budget transparency. The climate dashboard proves the concept.
5. **It builds the CSO's case for elevation.** The audit recommended elevating the CSO's role. A working accountability platform that shows cross-bureau performance gives the CSO a tool that substitutes for the line authority she doesn't have. If the tool works, it becomes evidence that the CSO's office should be permanently resourced and empowered.
